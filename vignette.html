Vignette

Access to healthcare in the United States varies widely accross geographic areas. While national statistics often suggest enough healthcare, county-level data reveals the substantial inequalities in healthcare workforce availability, hospital infrastructure, and socioeconomic conditions that shape access to the type of care one would receive.

The Healthcare Access and Equity Explorer is designed to explore data analysis of county-level healthcare access indicators in the United States. This would be most useful towards undergraduate students studying public health, biology, or policy. Some researchers who are conducting preliminary analysis. And even policymakers that want to gain a greater understanding of geographic healthcare disparities.

This app helps us answer questions such as how does access to healthcare providers vary across US counties or are counties with higher poverty or uninsured rates associated with lower healthcare capacity. This app is designed as an interactive tool that allows its users to visualize these patterns and relationships.

Required packages/Versions used by the App

The Shiny app use the following R packages: shiny, tidyverse, DT, sf, tigris, leaflet, viridis, and bslib.

Default Data Source

The app uses county-level data from the Area Health Resources Files by the Health Resources and Services Administration. The years we are looking at are from 2021-2023. https://data.hrsa.gov/data/download

Workflow

The Univariate Analysis tab allows users to look at the distribution a a single healthcare or socioeconomic variable across all US counties. There is a variable selector, histogram slider, and optional log scale transformation. Below we have a screenshot of the app with the Univariate Analysis showing the median family income. This helps us show the overall variation. This particular histogram shows the more normal distribution where many are in the middle and only few are very very low and few are very very high.

The Bivariate analysis tab allows users to look at the relationships between two variables. They have a x axis variable selection, y axis variable selection, optional log transformations, and optional linear trend line. The graph will show a scatterplot of the selected variables. For example below we have the relationship between poverty rate percentage and the percent being uninsured under 65. The trend shows that as the poverty percentage goes up so does the uninsured rate. The mapping tab allows users to look at the geographic variation in healthcare. There is a variable selection and the choice to cap extreme values that are in the 95th percentile which would be helpful in order to not get skewed results. Below is the active physician count. Most have low active physician counts but we can see they skyrocket where cities would be. The data table provides full access to the cleaned dataset.
